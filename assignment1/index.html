What was the first thing you paid attention to when interacting with the experience?

I had a few seconds of screen loading before being surprised with the lovely theme of the website with its subtle pastel gradient colour scheme. I was impressed by its interesting 3D elements displayed on the website.

As I realised the 3D models are the main subjects leading the primary style of this website as well, I was completely amazed and compelled to spend time experiencing more. Since it was 3D, I made sure I had the right choice of moving around and would not miss out anything by moving the cursor around to navigate the next move and get a sense of this digital space. I quickly sensed the movements and noticed that if I hovered around the 3D modeling subjects, the items on each subject slightly jumped up as a welcoming signal for me to click on them for further interaction.

Question 2: Spend two minutes with the experience and create a list of each of your discrete actions.

  • Navigating menu
  • Back to homepage
  • Interacting with 3D elements
  • Hovering around
  • Clicking on one of the 3D elements
  • Exploring Career page

Firstly, I selected the menu on the upper right for my first navigation as I wanted to get familiar with the environment. The sidebar appeared with some sections for me to look through. After I roughly knew what this website offered in general, I went back to Homepage and hovered around. I randomly clicked on some 3D items as they were seemingly interactive and potentially built with intentions. Some of them stood still but then I was surprised and impressed when I clicked on the sofa item, the pen popped up and the laptop was closed. Similarly, I clicked on it again, and it closed. I kept moving the cursor and saw the 3D models slightly moving in space. I clicked on one of the subjects which led me to the Career page. I kept doing the same thing as I did when I was moved to this new environment - hovering around and seeing the models bobbing back and forth. I clicked on 'Discover Career' button and it guided me to another page to read more information and view more images. Here I could scroll down and get informed.

Question 3: What part of the experience did you spend the most time engaging with?

I found the 3D elements very captivating so I spent time clicking on them again and again to see their potential movements instead of much intention of seeing what was included as the purpose of the models.

For example, there were five main subjects/models/stages on the Homepage, as I hovered around each of them, it popped up as a small interaction - the stage jumped up, a small ball and a coin slowly spinned in the air, or the sofa floated, etc. I was into exploring the features of all 3D models to see the implications and the design, because even the shades of the subjects were designed meticulously with moving interactions. Observing them also made me get the sense of the environment more as I scrolled down more and more. Since they integrated the 3D models with specific interactions, I enjoyed playing around with them. On the Omada page, I could actually throw the ball into the basket or shoot the ball into the goal and score some points.

Question 4: What was the most common action in your two minute interaction with the experience?

The most common actions: Hovering and clicking. I continued my experience by moving the cursor until seeing something popping up that I could click on and explore further.

For example when I was redirected to the Luni Life page, there was a button with the colour of luminous green, which was the signal for me to definitely click on and be redirected to another page. There, I continued by clicking on the available photos to see what it offered and wanted to introduce. Hovering helped me see the subtle movements of the subjects.

Question 5: What is your impression of the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

As I experienced it, I found out the intended primary goal of the website is to create a playable interactive space to provide the introduction to Luni and hire employees. Its main aim is to welcome new talents to join them after exploring the presentation of their brand via entertaining design elements. Through the interactive experience, I got to know what Luni offers, learn about their new app product and working environment engagingly and originally.

Question 6: How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

The website offers a wide range of impressive interactions to go through all of the introductions about Luni. It has smooth and entertaining interactivity and simple navigation to explore all sections either in order or randomly. The space is full of creativity which represents well the purpose of their products and truly displays the goal of Luni as an app producing brand, specifically as an employer. The 3D elements welcome the users to its world and feel involved while navigating through. Moreover, it’s less in text to highlight one main big title, clear visuals and intentionally leads to another page for more text and further information, which avoids confusion and brings effective information consumption. The main purpose, hiring, is demonstrated on the homepage, which is placed on top and separated from other elements.

Question 7: What is your impression of how the experience should be interacted with over time? (For how long and how many different times)

The website is designed for people who seek recruitment from Luni so they might need multiple visits. It offers updating status of career opportunities which is appealing for the employees to revisit and keep an eye on throughout the time to check for vacant positions or hiring locations. However, other information is fixed and can be explored in one visit. Saying that, the interactive experience is engaging and captivating enough to let people desire to navigate around once more when they come to the website again.

Question 8: How does the interactive experience communicate how it should be interacted with over time?

On top of the website, there is specifically a statement of how many talents they are looking for, next to the menu bar. So before the users ever navigate through the site, they can quickly learn about the career opportunities. This would decide on the next users’ experience, either exit to revisit next time or keep exploring. The number of the talents they are seeking is displayed in another box colour, which manipulates people’s mind that this number would change now and then and encourages people to revisit to check for updates.

Question 9: What other media forms (digital or otherwise) does the experience reference?

The playful 3D design elements as a main theme of the website reminded me of video games. The way I could directly interact with the elements made me feel like I was in a game and exploring the site as if I was going through game levels. When reading the main text, information about Luni, it evoked a sense of reading an online article with well-structured text in paragraphs and clear images and typography, which enhance the readability and engagement. As for specifically the Luni Life page, it might refer to Behance or other online portfolio sites to display art and products. It also reminded me of browsing an online shopping website where I need to go through every single product.

As for the interaction with 3D elements, they were so lively that I wanted to tap on them to see what might happen like playing games. Even clicking on the stages as if I was completing one level and going on the next one. To read the main information, I was more careful and spent more time going through rather than some quick interactions because it gave me a sense of reading articles. As for the sense of exploring online galleries/portfolio sites, the website encouraged me to linger over their products and project descriptions. It’s a way to learn about what they have done. It also made me choose what project I wanted to further explore based on the thumbnail picture and then browsed the website as if I was going through the categories when doing online shopping.

Question 11: What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should feel when engaging with your research experience?

The visual styles of game themes made me feel a sense of excitement, enhancing enjoyment. Then I slowed down and felt informed with the readings. As an expectation of a visually compelling space, the gallery-like page evoked feelings of inspiration and admiration while navigating through and it also made me feel more curious about what this space could offer. Along with the feeling of browsing shopping websites, it brought the feeling of engagement to explore the products with high-quality images and detailed descriptions.

Question 12: What is the most frustrating part of the interaction to you and what makes that part frustrating?

The experience was mostly satisfying and worth the time as the website was designed in detail and had a gentle colour scheme to stay for a long while and explore. However, the longer I had the interaction, the more uncomfortable I got with it because the interaction was slow and had the effect of floating on everything. Even the scrolling down of the website would not be as smooth as I expected but having the sense of loading instead. It took time to move around the environment and required patience. Moreover, I scrolled down and waited for the slow-moving interaction with the hope of discovering a new section of the website but it only displayed the same as I could click on the top of the Homepage - which I have already done.

Question 13: What is the most satisfying part of the interaction to you and what makes that part satisfying?

The most satisfying part of the interaction was the interaction with the details of the 3D modelling elements. I was amazed and impressed by how different and unexpected each item could respond to when I interacted with it. I liked the way it popped up and felt everything lively instead of merely the sense of hardware with eye-catching visuals. Moreover, the interactions were moderate which means they did not make me feel like missing something or overwhelmed because of too much. Not every subject has the movement so it was very enough. Some of the interactions were designed more in-depth with positive responses and realistic dynamism.
